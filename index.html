Scene 2 / Scrub the seasons

Every bird is a row. Every month is a tile.

This is the core visualization: a wall of motion. Each row is one tagged stork, each column is a month from 2013 to 2019, and each tile's color is that bird's median motion "pulse" — an activity index derived from the tri-axial accelerometer. Brighter tiles are months of stronger body motion.

Drag the month scrubber to move a beam of attention through the study period, or click any bird row to open that individual's story below. Hover a tile for its derived sample value.

Hollow coral terminus = recorded death. Empty dark tiles = no sampled activity; they are not an outcome.

Scene 3 / Enter one bird

One stork is not a row. It is a rhythm.

Selecting a bird folds the flock down to a single life. The timeline shows that stork's median monthly pulse across the whole study; the clock shows its average activity by hour of day. Brush a stretch of the timeline to scale that rhythm by the pulse sampled in the chosen interval.

Project writeup

How the instrument works.

Question 1

What have we done so far?

We built a working explorable explanation, The Sky Has a Pulse, deployed on GitHub Pages. We took the public Movebank LifeTrack White Stork SW Germany dataset and processed its tri-axial accelerometer files into a per-bird, per-month motion "pulse" index, exported as a single JSON asset that the page loads at runtime.

Five coordinated scenes now share a single month beam: a canvas night sky breathes with the 20 sampled signals, the pulse wall shows every monthly tile and marks recorded deaths with severed coral endpoints, a selected-bird timeline can be brushed into its hourly rhythm, the death trails gather the twelve recorded endings, and the flock view places individual lines against the available-bird average. Selecting a bird or moving time in one scene ripples through all of them.

Question 2

What will be the most challenging part to design, and why?

The hardest problem is making a derived, abstract quantity feel honest and legible. Our "pulse" is not a directly observed value — it is an activity index computed from accelerometer bursts, and the current data is a systematic every-500th-row sample. A reader cannot intuitively check it the way they could check a dot on a map, so we have to design the encoding and annotations so the metric is understood rather than guessed at, without implying a precision the sample does not have.

A second challenge is the emotional "disappearance" scene. Encoding recorded death is central to our story, but it must inform without becoming morbid or decorative, and the color and motion cues must stay distinguishable from the activity encoding so the two systems are never confused. Tuning the scrubber, hover targets, and scroll transitions keeps the whole piece calm rather than busy on both a laptop and a phone.
