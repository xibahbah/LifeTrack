The Sky
Has a Pulse

Six years of white stork bio-logging data, told not as a route on a map but as bodily motion over time. Every stork carries an accelerometer; this is a reading of how their bodies move — and when they stop.

Scene 2 / Scrub the seasons

Every bird is a row. Every month is a tile.

This is the core visualization: a wall of motion. Each row is one tagged stork, each column is a month from 2013 to 2019, and each tile's color is that bird's median motion "pulse" — an activity index derived from the tri-axial accelerometer. Brighter tiles are months of stronger body motion.

Drag the month scrubber to move a beam of attention through the study period, or click any bird row to open that individual's story below. Hover a tile for the exact value.

Scene 4 / Feel disappearance

Some rhythms end.

Of the 20 birds in this sample, 12 deployments end in recorded death. The final version will mark those endings directly in the pulse wall and let the reader feel the archive as a record of loss, not only of travel.

Project writeup

Where the prototype stands.

Question 1

What have we done so far?

We have built a working prototype of our explorable explanation, The Sky Has a Pulse, deployed on GitHub Pages. We took the public Movebank LifeTrack White Stork SW Germany dataset and processed its tri-axial accelerometer files into a per-bird, per-month motion "pulse" index, exported as a single JSON asset that the page loads at runtime.

The page is laid out as a five-scene scrollytelling article matching our proposal storyboard. The central scene — the pulse wall — is a fully working D3.js visualization: a heatmap of 20 birds by 70 months built with D3 data joins and scales, a month scrubber that moves a beam across the study period, click-to-select on any bird row, and hover tooltips. Selecting a bird drives a second linked D3 scene with that individual's monthly timeline and 24-hour rhythm clock. The remaining scenes (the animated hook, the disappearance scene, and the flock summary) are scaffolded with their descriptive text and clearly marked as in progress.

Question 2

What will be the most challenging part to design, and why?

The hardest problem is making a derived, abstract quantity feel honest and legible. Our "pulse" is not a directly observed value — it is an activity index computed from accelerometer bursts, and the current data is a systematic every-500th-row sample. A reader cannot intuitively check it the way they could check a dot on a map, so we have to design the encoding and annotations so the metric is understood rather than guessed at, without implying a precision the sample does not have.

The second challenge is the emotional "disappearance" scene. Encoding recorded death is central to our story, but it must inform without becoming morbid or decorative, and the color and motion cues must stay distinguishable from the activity encoding so the two systems are never confused. Tuning the scrubber, the hover targets, and the scroll transitions so the whole piece feels calm rather than busy — on both a laptop and a phone — is the main work left before the final deliverable.
