The shorter way south

Every line is a real GPS track. The map begins in southwest Germany, then follows white storks as they move through France, Spain, North Africa, and the western Sahel.

The old instinct still points toward Africa. But many birds now stop closer to home.

1 / Departure

A field in Germany becomes a launch point.

In late summer, the flock leaves a tight nesting region near Lake Constance. The cream dots are current positions at the selected day; they stay small so the flock never becomes a white blob.

2 / The funnel

The routes pull southwest.

Gold trails mark the early season. As the scrubber moves into autumn, later segments turn orange and rust, showing the migration unfold rather than summarizing it all at once.

3 / The split

Two winter strategies appear on the same map.

Rust routes continue into Africa. River-teal routes stop in Spain or nearby western Europe and North Africa. The color switch is the main story: a long migration is being shortened.

4 / Closer to home

Spain becomes a wintering landscape.

Short-stopping is not a decorative cluster. It is geography: birds remain around the Iberian Peninsula instead of crossing the Sahara, making the survival tradeoff visible as distance on a map.

5 / The older path

A smaller group keeps crossing.

Follow an individual route with your cursor. The highlighted path is the only glow; the rest of the flock stays quiet so single-bird stories can be read without losing the geography.
